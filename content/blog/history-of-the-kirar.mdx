The Kirar is a bowl-shaped lyre, typically strung with six strings, found across
Ethiopian secular and spiritual music. It belongs to a broader family of East
African lyres that includes the Begena — its larger, deeper-toned relative used
almost exclusively in Ethiopian Orthodox devotional music — and shares that
instrument's box-and-crossbar construction, even as its playing style and
repertoire differ.

## A shared history

Lyres of this general family have been played across the Horn of Africa for
generations, passed down primarily through oral tradition rather than written
notation. The Kirar in particular found its place at celebrations, in secular
music, and in accompaniment to singers — a more portable, more melodically
flexible counterpart to the Begena's slow, meditative role in church.

## Why it's taught by ear

Because the tradition has always been carried player to player, most Kirar
technique is still best learned by watching, listening, and repeating — the
same way it has always been passed down. That's the model Abelkirar is built
on: structured lessons, but rooted in the way the instrument has always been
taught.

## Why it matters now

The Ethiopian and Eritrean Orthodox diaspora is large and growing, spread
across cities in the United States, the United Kingdom, and Europe. Passing
the Kirar to the next generation now means teaching it somewhere it can reach
that diaspora — which is exactly why Abelkirar exists.
